The tech sector is known for its fast-paced, high-pressure environment. Our methodology uses goals and deadlines to boost productivity. We fail fast. We iterate. We improve. Product development is a race, and we must be in a continual sprint if we want to outpace our competitors.

While we're always pushing to innovate and adapt, it's essential to remember that our minds need as much care and attention as our projects. The mental health of our colleagues should never take a backseat.

Burnt-out developers aren't good developers. Crunch time does not make good software. The odd all-nighter might be fun, but as the norm, you will quickly hit a wall. Software development, and the disciplines that surround it, demand mental wellness. You have to hold a lot of context in your head and come up with creative solutions. If you're tired, stressed or burnt out, you will soon struggle.

What if relaxed developers actually make good developers?

A wise project manager once told me that a task will always stretch to its deadline. Without deadlines, we might never release anything. There is truth to this, but it's often taken too far. A deadline as an objective can serve as intrinsic motivation if the person working towards it appreciates its value. Aggressive deadlines can leave people disillusioned and can negatively impact productivity, especially when they cause more meetings and distractions.

So, how do you create a productive and mentally supportive environment? Foster a culture of emotional safety and concentrate on empowering individuals. Don't bombard your team with directions; show them the destination, give them the tools to get there, and importantly, get out of their way. The result? Emotional fulfilment in the collective success of the company.

But we are all human, and regardless of your environment, there will always be hard times. That's why it's so important to encourage a culture of openness and support. It has to be okay to say "I'm struggling" without fear of judgment.

Switching off is vital. We're always connected, always 'on' — but our minds need to recharge. Find something that helps you relax and make time for it. It's better to perform consistently than to have periods of crunch time followed by burnout. Our bodies and minds aren't designed to be at 100% all the time.

When running on empty, it can be hard to identify that you're struggling. You wouldn't wait until your car's tank was empty before filling it with petrol, so why wait until you're burnt out to seek help?

We techies like to think of ourselves as progressive, so let's continue to quash the stigma around mental health. Let's commit to asking "are you okay?" and actually listening to the answer. Let's acknowledge that mental health matters, and that supporting one another is not just the right thing to do, but vital for our collective success.
