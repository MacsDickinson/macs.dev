One thing you quickly learn as a leader in tech is that people are not all the same. Strategies that work wonders for one individual can spell disaster for another. We are all unique, with our own beliefs, drivers, and motivations. This is what makes leading people, and line management in general, such an enriching experience.

But when does individuality cross the line into neurodiversity? Does fidgeting mean you have ADHD? If crowded spaces overwhelm you, do you have autism? Aren't we all "a little OCD" at times?

Short answer: no.

Conditions such as autism, ADHD, dyslexia and OCD are considered disabilities, diagnosed because of the sustained negative impact they are having on that person's life. Recently, there has been a flurry of media coverage about the over-diagnosis of ADHD. It's important to remember that behind each of these diagnoses is a person. A person who has been pushed to their limit, grappling with the challenge of fitting into societal norms. While over-diagnosis and the unregulated industry surrounding it is a problem, this should not be used to downplay the very real struggles of those currently grappling with these conditions.

People who know me know it's pretty obvious I have ADHD. I've been told I probably do, and I'm on the waiting list for diagnosis. That waiting list is three years. I've applied to be seen privately and my GP is being super supportive, but as anyone who's been through ADHD diagnosis knows, it's a real struggle to complete when you have ADHD. Case in point: I should be completing my assessment right now, not writing this post.

But there are two sides to this neurodiverse coin. While my preparation might leave a lot to be desired, I can think on my feet. My struggle to control my attention is an ongoing internal battle, but when I manage to focus on something, I truly hyperfocus. And here's the important bit: that's fine.

Contrary to what the education system in the UK suggests, we don't all need to be the same. In fact, we thrive when we aren't. Why have we been desperately trying to improve diversity in tech for over a decade? Is it just to tick some DE&I box, or is it because study after study shows that more diverse teams produce better results?

When we start to view our teams as individuals rather than resources, we unlock their true superpowers. By allowing each individual to use their strengths, we create a team that is greater than the sum of its parts. As a leader, building a team that compensates for your weaknesses lets you shine in your strengths.

This approach isn't exclusive to neurodiverse individuals. Focusing on nurturing the strengths of your team is inclusive by nature. Given the right environment, we all have the potential to become superheroes.
