Most organisations are designed using the structures that are easiest to draw: org charts, job titles, headcount. None of these reliably explain what an organisation can actually do — or why it struggles when things change.

That gap has bothered me for years, through restructures at Flutter and building a bank at LHV. So I've been writing my thinking down as a framework: Atomic Team Design.

The core idea is to reason about organisations through the lens of capability. Skills, knowledge and experience determine what an organisation can deliver. Not roles. Not titles. Not headcount.

ATD borrows its language from science. Atoms are single capabilities — a skill, a discipline, a practice. Roles bundle many capabilities together and go stale quickly; capabilities are composable, transferable, and evolve independently of structure. Two people with the same title may share few atoms. Two people with different titles may share many.

Molecules are compositions of atoms, and they come in two kinds: people, each a unique combination of capabilities, and services — the products and systems that require capabilities to operate. The relationship between the two is where it gets useful. People have capabilities; services require them. The gap between the two is your organisational risk.

Cells are teams — the primary unit of structure, where capability meets work. A cell's purpose defines what it needs to do, which defines the capabilities it requires, which defines the people who should be part of it. Purpose first, composition second.

Organs are groups of related cells, and the organism is the organisation as a whole. The biological metaphor is deliberate. Machines are designed once and repaired by swapping parts; living systems grow, adapt and compensate. Organisations behave far more like the latter.

Which leads to the principle I care most about: change through recomposition, not reorganisation. Reorganisation is the big-bang restructure — everyone gets a new box on the chart, and it's disruptive, expensive and often traumatic. Recomposition is continuous small adjustments: people move between cells as needs shift, cells gain or shed services as purpose evolves.

Recomposition is maintenance. Reorganisation is surgery. You should need surgery rarely, and be maintaining constantly. If you're reorganising every year, you're not designing — you're reacting.

One more thing: constraints — regulation, cognitive load, system boundaries, risk tolerance — aren't obstacles to work around. They're inputs that should actively shape how teams are built. Working in a bank teaches you that quickly.

The full framework lives at atomicteamdesign.com. Start small: map the capabilities you have and the ones you need, then ask of each team — can you articulate why it exists?
