Kent Beck and Beth Andres-Beck wrote a piece last year that's been playing on my mind. Forest & Desert - the idea that software teams aren't all playing the same game. Some are building in fertile conditions; others are surviving under an oppressive sun.

I've been thinking about what happens when you introduce agentic AI into both environments.

Let's imagine crop farming has just been invented. In the forest, it thrives. The soil is rich, the rainfall reliable, the conditions already right. Plant seeds and things grow.

When folks in the desert catch wind of this advancement they try it and it fails. Not because farming is broken. But because the conditions aren't there. You need to invest in irrigation first. You need to treat the soil. Only then does farming deliver what it promised.

The same is true for agentic engineering.

Teams with strong test coverage, modular architecture, and tight feedback loops are seeing the benefits. AI amplifies what's already there. It moves fast because there's a safety net to catch mistakes, and a clear signal when something goes wrong.

Teams without those foundations try the same tools and hit walls. The output isn't reliable enough to ship at scale. Or the bottleneck just moves to planning, to review, to all the places where process debt has been quietly accumulating for years.

Agentic AI is a multiplier. And multiplying a desert doesn't give you a forest.

The good news? You can terraform. But it takes significant investment:

- → Automated test coverage you actually trust
- → CI/CD that gives you confidence to ship
- → Observability so you know when things break
- → Cycle time you measure and actively shrink

Only when you can move smoothly from idea to feedback consistently and reliably should you introduce AI into the mix.

Build the conditions first. The tools will follow.
