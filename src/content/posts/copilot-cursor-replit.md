At work, we've adopted GitHub Copilot over the past few months. Feedback has been broadly positive, with one engineer likening it to having a keen junior developer always on hand. However, its impact hasn't been transformational.

On personal projects, I've been using Cursor for the last month and found it to be a significant improvement over Copilot in predicting my next steps. For someone like me — a leader who hasn't coded full-time in nearly a decade — having an IDE that remembers all the basic syntax I've forgotten is a game changer. While iteration is necessary, it accelerates the initial stages, especially when you're a bit rusty.

Over the weekend, I took Replit for a spin, and it was eye-opening. Replit goes beyond being an AI assistant in your IDE; it has an AI agent that can understand relatively sizeable requirements, update your codebase, test, iterate on issues and push its changes to GitHub. I had a simple football stats site built and deployed in 40 minutes, and added a bunch more features throughout the evening. The pace was insane.

That said, it wasn't without challenges. The AI struggled with some features, and not because of complexity. It was able to add caching and a refresh strategy but struggled to add a dark mode toggle. There were a few things like this where it was quicker to write it myself. The initial implementation was slow and poorly architected — but to its credit, once I explained why and outlined a better structure, it adapted quickly. This tool feels perfect for rapid prototyping, but I wouldn't rely on it yet to create maintainable, production-ready code.

A word of caution: Replit's $25 subscription only includes $25 in AI usage credits, and I burned through $20 of that over the weekend. Sure, it's still a fraction of the cost of a human developer, but you need to commit plenty of your own time guiding it.

The market for AI developer tools is heating up with the rise of agentic AI. The global market for AI in software development is projected to grow at a compound annual rate of over 20% in the next few years, driven by the promise of faster cycles, reduced costs, and tools that increasingly mimic the collaborative experience of working with another developer.

The pace of innovation in this space is impressive, underpinned by the LLM models that power these tools. If the current trajectory holds, we may soon see agents capable of producing cost-effective and maintainable solutions. Until then, I'll be sticking with Cursor and keeping an eye on how Replit and others evolve.

And for anyone stressing about AI replacing them as software engineers — remember, coding is just part of what we do. The experience gained supporting crappy code and working with clients who don't know what they need is hard to replicate with AI.
